# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

People auditing **their own** digital footprint — technically literate individuals
(developers, security-aware professionals, privacy-conscious people) who suspect
their data is scattered across breaches, leaks, and public records and want a
precise, trustworthy read on their exposure plus concrete fixes. They arrive
wary: they've seen creepy "people-search" sites and fear-mongering security ads,
and they want a tool that respects them. Context: a focused session to find out
what's exposed and shut it down — not idle browsing.

## Product Purpose

Arescope is a **personal exposure scanner + privacy remediation engine**. The user
provides identifiers they own (name, username, email, photo, IP); Arescope runs
them through OSINT sources, an LLM judges each finding by real-world severity
(info → critical), and it proposes concrete fixes for the dangerous ones. The
goal is to **shrink the user's footprint, not investigate others.** Success =
the user leaves knowing exactly what's exposed, how bad each item actually is,
and what to do about it — with measurably less exposure after acting.

The website's job (this brand surface): communicate that value with enough craft
and restraint that a wary, technical visitor trusts it on sight and joins the
waitlist / starts a scan.

## Brand Personality

**Precise technical instrument.** Three words: *precise, restrained, trustworthy.*
Voice is terse and technical — no fluff, no hype, no fear. It reads like a
well-built tool made by people who know the domain. Confidence comes from craft
and clarity, not adjectives. Closest reference in *feel* (not literal look):
Raycast — fast, considered, every detail deliberate, premium without shouting.
Emotional goal: quiet confidence and control ("this is well-built; I'm in
capable hands"), never anxiety.

## Anti-references

This site must **not** look or feel like any of these:

1. **Creepy OSINT / hacker tool** — terminal-green, dark-web aesthetic, "we found
   EVERYTHING about them," surveillance / people-search framing. Arescope audits
   *your own* data; the design must make that unmistakable and never voyeuristic.
2. **Fear-mongering security vendor** — red-alert FUD walls, "YOU ARE AT RISK!!!",
   scare-to-sell urgency, trust-badge/certification soup. We inform calmly; we
   never weaponize anxiety.
3. **Generic templated SaaS** — Inter + blurple gradient hero, identical rounded
   feature cards, the default AI-generated startup look. Commit to a distinct
   direction instead.
4. **Cluttered / busy enterprise** — wall-of-features density, many competing
   CTAs, heavy nav/chrome. Stay clean, fast, focused.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice what you preach.** A privacy product must itself be exemplary on
   privacy: self-hosted fonts/assets (no third-party CDN calls), no trackers, no
   leaking the visitor's data to make a point. The site is evidence of the ethos.
2. **Calm over alarm.** Severity is communicated with precision and restraint, not
   red panic. The user should feel informed and in control, never frightened.
3. **Self-audit, made unmistakable.** Every framing reinforces "this is *your*
   data, under *your* control" — never "investigate anyone." This is the moat and
   the ethics; surface it, don't bury it.
4. **Craft signals competence.** For a wary technical audience, visible polish and
   speed *are* the trust argument. Restraint, precision, and fast load do more
   than any testimonial.
5. **Show, don't tell.** Demonstrate the judgement (severity, ranked fixes)
   concretely rather than claiming "AI-powered." Let the mechanism speak.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text ≥ 4.5:1 contrast, large text ≥ 3:1 (verified
against the near-black background — no light-gray-for-elegance failures). Full
`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` support (motion is enhancement, never required
to understand content). Don't encode meaning in color alone — severity uses
label + position + shape, not just hue (also helps color-blind users). Keyboard-
navigable, visible focus states, semantic landmarks.
